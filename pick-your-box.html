Pick Your Box

The Nomadik is originally a subscription-based outdoor gear company that aims to deliver new, high-quality gear from best-in-class brands in themed boxes to help motivate customers to go on their next adventure.

Role

UX Designer, Developer

Duration

2 weeks

Problem

Having recently transferred from one eCommerce Platform to another, The Nomadik looked to restructure and redesign their monthly subscription page in order to help people save time, money, and create a memorable and smooth shopping experience for their customers.

Solution

To address user pain points and improve user experience we used data gathered through user testing and taking a look at competitors with a similar product format to perform a competitive analysis that helped us make informed design solutions.

How might we help users make a more informed decision when picking a subscription plan and increase business and revenue for the company?

What did I do?

Gathered insights by using data from:

  • Stakeholder Interviews
  • User Testing
  • Heuristic Evaluation
  • Competitive Analysis

Also designed/developed the following:

  • Low Fidelity Wireframes
  • Live Build

I collaborated extensively across teams to implement design solutions. The final result was a fresh, new user interface to learn about and/or purchase a monthly subscription plan.

Stakeholder Interviews

I met with the CEO, Marketing Coordinator and one of the heads of the Customer Experience team to better understand their hopes and goals for this redesign.

1- Increase Conversions

Increase customer acquisition by converting more visitors into subscribers.

2- Customer Confidence

Based on user feedback, it would help customers if we clarify value propositions and how the service works. Hopefully also decreasing tickets submitted to customer support.

3- Address Pain Points

Come up with a solution that better fits their new platform specifically

Heuristic Evaluation

To identify potential areas for improvement, I spent some time going through the usual user flow on this page to identify major issues. I structured my evaluation based on these following questions:

  • Will the user notice that the correct action is available to them?
  • Will the user associate the correct action with the outcome they expect to achieve?
  • If the correct action is performed; will the user see that progress is being made towards their intended outcome?

1. No clear product explanation

When customers first arrive at this page they are immediately told how to make an order, but not so much what the product is and how the service works.

2. Confusing pricing module

When reaching this section, as a user at first glance you may think the prices you see on the left are what you pay up front, but it's actually the finer text on the right side. A lot of customers don't notice this and it causes a lot of abandoned checkouts and orders.

2. No chance to review order

Selecting a subscription plan then displays a pop up modal where they select their first box theme. The modal doesn't give a detailed breakdown of what comes in the box, and once customers select one it immediately takes them to checkout without giving them a chance to review their order.

Competitive Analysis

With the same questions in mind during my Heuristic Evaluation, we looked at competitor design solutions and gathered what is applicable to our case.

We looked at several competitors or companies with similar products such as: ButcherBox, Mystery Tackle Box, Atlas Coffee Club, and Get Cairn to try and see what is giving them an edge and what gaps we have in our site.

Findings

  • Competitors have a clear description of how their product works and what customer's should expect to receive.
  • Some provide a step by step flow that shows customers the differences in plans and themes.
  • A pain point we experienced with several competitors: when we want to review our plan we get prompted to already give personal information before we're even sure we want to buy.
  • Some show an order summary at the end of their user flow before going into checkout.

Design Solutions

Based on the collected research and through close collaboration with the Marketing Coordinator and continuous feedback from the CEO we ended up with the following design solutions in an effort to improve customer experience and increasing revenue.

1. Revamped landing

Improved the hero and section above the fold that includes a testimonial from a magazine feature that briefly explains what the product is about. We give users an option to just go straight to the selection process if they so choose.

2. More detailed product description and value proposition

Added a new section right below the fold where we go into detail of the value proposition and what to expect from the product/service.

3. Step by step selection process

Changed the plan and theme selection process in order to help the customer better understand what they are choosing and to help make sure they get the outcome they expected. Also emphasized the price they pay upfront instead of the per box payment to less confusing.

4. Order Summary

Added an Order Summary section that goes over what the customer has chosen and gives them the opportunity to change themes or plans before moving on. From here as well, clicking the button takes them to the platform's default cart page which is also another check in case they have other products in their cart to review their order in full.

Results

After completing a working build and pushing it live. I set up a rerouting test that splits the website traffic 50/50 on mobile between our design solution vs. the original flow.

Statistics

  • 72% increase on time spent on page.
  • 15% increase in conversion rate

Reflections

Designing for the product that is the company's main source of revenue requires a level of discipline, thoroughness, and dedication to learning that is hard to experience outside of a professional setting. Every design detail is thoroughly reviewed and questioned, often in multiple rounds by stakeholders and members of other teams. Often I found myself advocating for the users, and for myself as the developer, combining that and what I learned from the others with a more business minded approach to find what is technically feasible, desirable, and profitable.
